How to Post to All Social Media at Once: A UK Tradesperson's Guide (2026)

Practical guide for builders, plumbers, electricians and local service firms. Reading time about 7 minutes.

You finished a tidy job, the customer is happy, and the photos look great. Then the day runs away from you and the post you meant to put up never happens. Multiply that by every job, and the result is a brilliant tradesperson who looks quiet online while the firm down the road, who does worse work, looks busy.

This guide shows you exactly how to write one update about a job and get it onto all your social channels at once, in under a minute, from your phone. By the end you will have a simple, repeatable routine that keeps you visible without stealing the evenings you would rather spend with your family.

The honest truth: the thing stopping most tradespeople from winning more work online is not a lack of marketing skill. It is a lack of time. Fix the time problem and the visibility problem fixes itself.

What you need before you start

  • A phone with your social accounts already set up (Facebook, Instagram, and whichever others you use)
  • A way to take a quick photo or two on the job
  • A cross-posting tool so you write once and it goes everywhere. Blast Everything is built for exactly this and aimed at UK trades
  • Time needed per post once you are set up: under a minute

Why posting to one platform is not enough

Different customers live on different platforms. An older homeowner might find you on Facebook or Google. A landlord might check LinkedIn. A younger couple doing up their first house might scroll Instagram or TikTok. If you only post to one place, everyone on the other channels never sees that you exist, or worse, sees an account that has been quiet for months and assumes you have packed it in.

Posting everywhere used to mean writing the same thing five times and reformatting it for each app. That is the bit nobody has time for. The fix is to write once and let a tool handle the rest.

Step by step: post one update everywhere

Step 1. Take the photo while you are still on site. The best time to capture a job is the moment it is finished and clean. A wide shot of the finished work and one close-up detail is plenty. Photos taken later, or not at all, are the main reason posts never go up.
Step 2. Write one plain-English update. No marketing voice. Just say what you did and where, roughly. For example: "Finished a full bathroom refit in Newbury this week. New suite, retiled floor to ceiling, sorted the old leak under the bath while we were at it. Happy customer." That is it.
Step 3. Drop it into your cross-posting tool. Open Blast Everything, paste or type your update, and add the photos. This is the one place you enter the content.
Step 4. Let it format for each platform. The same update should read naturally on each channel rather than being one bland message pasted everywhere. A good tool handles this for you so a Facebook post does not look like a LinkedIn post by accident.
Step 5. Choose where it goes. Tick the channels you want. Most of the time that is all of them. You are not forced to post everywhere every time, but the point is that posting to all eleven is now as quick as posting to one.
Step 6. Tap post. One tap and you are live across the lot, before you have loaded the tools back into the van.

How often should a tradesperson post?

You do not need to post every day. A realistic, sustainable rhythm wins over a burst that fizzles out:

If you areAim forWhat to post
A sole trader, flat out1 to 2 posts a weekOne finished job, one tip or behind-the-scenes
A small team2 to 3 posts a weekFinished jobs, before and after, the crew at work
Growing and quoting hard3 to 4 posts a weekJobs, reviews, the areas you cover, who you are

The trick is that when posting takes under a minute, hitting these numbers stops feeling like a chore.

What to post when you have not got a finished job photo

  • A before-and-after from an older job you never shared
  • A quick tip homeowners actually want (how to spot a dodgy roof, when to bleed radiators)
  • The areas you cover, so local searchers know you serve them
  • A short note when you have a slot open next week
  • A genuine thank you when a customer leaves a kind review

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Waiting until you are home. By then the moment, and the photo, are gone. Capture on site.
  • Sounding like an advert. Customers trust a normal human voice far more than slogans.
  • Posting to one platform only. You are invisible to everyone on the others.
  • Going quiet for months. A stale account looks worse than no account. Little and often wins.

Bottom line: the firms that win the most local work are rarely the best on the tools. They are the ones who stay visible. Make posting take under a minute and staying visible stops being hard.
